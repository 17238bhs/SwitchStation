Why Mechanical Keyboards?
With the rise the digital era, keyboards were made as one of the main ways we have of interacting with technology. Yet despite being such important devices which many people spend hours a day in front of, they are often brushed aside as nothing worth paying attention to. In fact, despite our society making numerous advancements in technology in almost every field, keyboards are actually one of the things which we have downgraded in over time-favoring cheapness and mass production over high quality.
What are Mechanical Keyboards?
Mechanical keyboards are devices which use mechanical key switches to register key presses. This differs from most common keyboards seen today, which use a "membrane" that uses a linked pressure pad to register keypresses, rather than individual switches.
The World of Custom Keyboards
When it comes to custom keyboards, the main benifits come from the improved typing feel, sound, and aesthetic. Thanks to it being custom, there is an almost unlimited amount of ways for people to customise their keyboards in the exact way they want. Custom keyboards are almost entirely community made, with new people in the community designing and making new options almost every day.
Parts of a Mechanical Keyboard
Switches
Switches put the ‘mechanical’ in ‘mechanical keyboards’ and make up the backbone of this entire passion. These little mechanisms have just as much character as keycaps and cases, despite being largely unseen, and understanding what goes into them is important to understanding how keyboards work. A keyboard switch is the little component that sits underneath the keycap and connects to the keyboard PCB. On a mechanical keyboard, every key has its own switch. Switches vary in how they look, sound, and feel. Most of the time, you won't see them—but they play a big role in how your keyboard sounds and feels when you use it. Switches are generally categorized into 3 types that indicate their design: Tactile, Linear, and Clicky. Tactile: has a tactile bump during the keypress before bottom-out Linear: has an smooth keypress with no tactile bump Clicky: has a tactile bump and an audible click sound Switch Parts: Housing The housing includes the base housing and top housing. The base housing has two pins that slot into the PCB. They need to be soldered in most cases—if a keyboard is “hotswap,” that means soldering isn’t needed. Switches can be either plate mount or PCB mount: a PCB mount switch has two additional plastic legs that secure it into the PCB. Stem The stem sits within the switch housing and on the spring, while the ‘+’ extrusion locks into the bottom of a keycap. The two legs help determine the switch’s level of tactility as it comes into contact with the metal leaf. Spring The spring sits on the base housing pole and supports the stem’s vertical movement. Springs come in a wide range of weights, with heavier springs requiring more strength for a keypress. Metal Leaf When a switch is pressed, the stem presses into the metal leaf that sits in the bottom housing. The connection registers the keypress onto the PCB, allowing those magical symbols to show up on your computer. Modifying Switches Now that we’ve got the basics down on the core differences and parts that make up a particular switch, let’s have a quick look at the various ways switches are modified to further personalize them. Lube The first mod that enthusiasts often do, even on their first build, is applying a lubricant to switch components. Depending on the type of lube that is used, the switch feel, sound, and even weight can be drastically transformed. Switch Films Switch films are a thin film applied on the base of the switch and sandwiched between the base and the top housing. This tightens the fit between the two parts to reduce wobble, affecting the feel and sound. Springs Changing the stock spring in a switch is an easy way of changing the switch feel. Alternative springs come in different materials, weights, and even a progressive choice. - Keyboard University
Keycaps
Keycaps are the face of any keyboard and are one of the more impactful factors on sound, feel, look, and the overall experience of the board. These little rectangles pack in a tremendous amount of choice and consideration, so it’s important to understand the various factors before choosing how to outfit any build. Keycaps are the plastic caps that cover each individual key, and make up much of a keyboard’s aesthetics. They’re one of the main ways you can customize your keyboard. If you have a mechanical keyboard, switching out your keycaps is simply a matter of pulling them off your board and putting on new ones. Keycaps vary in size, shape, colour, and plastic, in addition to the characters and words printed on them. A keycap’s profile refers to its shape. Though many keycaps look similar in their top-down view, their height can vary and your fingers can feel the difference in shape.
Plate
In a mechanical keyboard, the plate is used to hold switches and keep them stable. Changing the material of the plate makes a big difference in both the sound and feel of the board. Examples of plate materials: - Polycarbonate (PC) This plate material provides the softest feel, along with a deep sound signature. -FR4 This material is the same one which PCBs are made from. They produce a deep sound and flexible feel, though typically not as much as PC -Aluminium This material is considered a middle ground in terms of feel and sound. -Brass This plate is extremely stiff and high pitched
Stabilizers
Stabilizers (often called 'stabs') are keyboard parts that prevent larger keys from wobbling when pressed, and help bind the keycap to the switch. They sit next to the keyboard switch to stabilize the keycap when you press it. Most keyboards require multiple stabilizers, since most will have multiple large keys. Keys that are 2u or larger need a stabilizer. These usually include keys like the shift keys, backspace, enter/return key, and spacebar.
PCB
The PCB (Printed Circuit Board) is the heart of the keyboard, fitting all of the needed electronics, as well as the switches on it. PCBs can come in either soldered or hotswap variants. Soldered requiring all switches to be soldered in to the board, and hotswap allowing switches to be easily placed on and taken off at any time. Some PCBs may support bluetooth, although it is extremely uncommon and seen only to budget keyboards.
Case
The case of the keyboard is extremely important to the identity of the keyboard, changing the feel, acoustics and of course, aesthetic of the keyboard. Higher end cases are typically all made with aluminium, although polycarbonate is also common as a side option. For cheaper boards, PC, ABS plastics as well as acrylic are all common materials for the case.
Putting a Mechanical Keyboard together
There are a range of different ways to mount the plate and pcb assembly to the case. This ends up having a large influence on the feel and often sound of the keyboard, with it being up to the keyboard designer to choose what mount to design the keyboard for.
